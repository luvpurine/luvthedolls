Marsha P. Johnson (1945-1992)

Johnson was a prominent activist during the 1960s and 1970s gay rights movement in New York City.
The "P" in her name stands for "Pay It No Mind," a motto of hers. Johnson began her activism during
the resistance at Stonewall, where she led many protests against the aforementioned police raid. While
being a part of the Gay Pride Parade, the Gay Liberation Front, and the Gay Activist Alliance; Johnson
wasn't afraid to bring light to the transphobia and racism within the movement. Alongside Sylvia Rivera, she
founded the Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries (STAR) to shelter transgender youth who were disowned by their families. Johnson's goal was "to see gay people liberated and free and to have equal rights that other people
have in America." And though she experienced hardship, Johnson moved greatly through this goal with a smile. In 1992, one of the worst years for anti-LGBTQ violence, Johnson's body was found in the Hudson River. Today she is remembered
as one of the most influential female and POC activists of all time.

Sylvia Rivera (1951-2002)
Sylvia Rivera was an advocate for transgender rights (especially those who were POC), especially during the Stonewall
Riots. Rivera ran away from home at 11 due to intense discrimination, and in 1963, she met Marsha P. Johnson, who changed
her life. Johnson and Rivera were both notable during the Stonewall Inn Uprising of 1969, an event that further fueled
Rivera's activist and revolutionary spirit. Stonewall led to increased gay rights movements across the nation, but Rivera
and other transgender individuals were often discouraged from participating. Rivera often spoke out and fought against this
discrimination, and she founded the Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries (STAR) to shelter transgender youth who were
disowned by their families alongside Johnson. Rivera left the New York activist scene after attempting suicide, but returned
after Johnson's death in 1992. She continued to fight for the inclusion of transgender people in the gay rights movement, started
the Transy House, was honored in the 25th Anniversary of Stonewall Inn, and continued to march until getting liver cancer.
Venus Xtravaganza (1965-1988)
Venus Xtravaganza was a prominent performer in the NYC ballroom scene. She began crossdressing and performing at 13-14 years old,
and eventually moved from her family's home to live with her grandmother in Jersey City to embrace her transness. The founder of
the voguing House of Xtravaganza, Hector Valle, invited Venus to join. After Valle's death, Angie Xtravaganza took over and house mother
and Venus was her drag daughter. Venus was one of the interviewees in voguing documentary Paris Is Burning, where she shared her goals of
being a model and getting gender-affirming surgery. Before the ending of the documentary's production, Venus' body was found under a mattress,
where she was strangled and murdered.
Shalimar Seiuli (1976-1998)
Shalimar Seiuli was an American Samoan public figure and dancer who identified as a fa'fafine (the third gender in Samoa) and
was an active member of the Mormon community. In her LDS life, she had to compromise her identity at times, but her family accepted
her fa'fafine label. As a teenager, Seiuli was comfortable with expressing her identity and was popular in her high school (captain of the cheerleading team). At 17, she won the Miss Island Queen Pageant, and she's remembered as one of the star performers of the
competition even today. Shalimar moved to California in 1996 to fufill her dreams of becoming a fashion designer and entertainer.
In 1997, Shalimar was involved in a scandal where she was arrested after being picked up by a vehicle driven by Eddie Murphy. While Murphy
insisted that he was uninvolved in the crime, a clip shows him and the police officers shaking hands during Shalimar's arrest, and the entertainer
stated that it was unfair that only she had gotten arrest. In the following year, Shalimar's body was found, and authorities claimed that she fell. However, there is evidence suggesting (heavily) that she was murdered, including a testimony from her loved ones.
Gwen Araujo (1985-2002)
Gwen Araujo was a 17-year-old girl from California who chose the name "Gwen" based off of pop star "Gwen Stefani." She was made fun of at school
for her voice and appearance after going through hormone replacement therapy. In fall of 2002, Gwen met Michael Magidson, Jose Merel,
Jaron Nabors, and Jason Cazares at a party where they smoked weed and had sex (which, was staturory rape by Magidson and Merel under
California law). On October 3rd 2002, Araujo was beat up, assaulted, and murdered by these four men after they found out she was trans.
Rita Hester (1963-1998)
Rita Hester was a 34-year-old woman who resided in Boston who was also a popular entertainer, dancer, and singer in Jacque's Cabaret. She's also
praised for her talent and promise as a poet by her loved ones. On November 28th 1998, Rita was found dead in her home, stabbed at least 20 times by an
unknown assilant.
Chanelle Pickett (1972-1995)
Chanelle Pickett and her twin sister Gabrielle are both transgender, and the young girls were abandoned by their mother after she learned this.
They were then placed into foster care. In adulthood, the sisters worked at a telephone company in Brookline, but then left after being harassed
by a coworker. Chanelle herself turned to sex work, as finding jobs as a transwoman proved difficult. In November of 1995, Chanelle was brutally murdered by
William Palmer, a man she met at the oldest gay bar in Boston, Playland Cafe. Palmer defended himself by using the idea of "trans panic defense",
an extenstion of "gay panic defense" where one tries to excuse their violence (often murder) against another because they were in shock of their homosexuality
or transness (which the police bought).
Monique Thomas (1963-1998)
Monique Thomas was a woman from Boston who attended Northeastern University's dentistry school. On September 11, 1998, Monique's body was found in
her apartment, a week after her murder by George Stallings.
Christine Jorgensen (1926-1989)
Christine Jorgensen was an American entertainer who was the first person to undergo a successful gender reassignment operation. She was often plagued
with gender dysphoria her entire life, and served in the military before going through therapy to transition. In 1952, Christine's operation was successful
and she became well-known because of it, gaining prominence and wealth as an entertainer. She died from bladder and lung cancer in 1989.
Roberta Cowell (1918-2011)
Roberta Cowell was a British woman who was a Spitfire pilot and prisoner of war in WWII. She became the first known British transwoman to undergo
sex reassignment surgery, receiving a vaginoplasty from Dr. Harold Gillies (who previously performed on transgender man Michael Dillon). In 1951, she
had her birth certificate changed to be female.
Miss Major Griffin-Gracy (1946-2025)
Miss Major first came out as transgender at 12-13 years old, where she became involved in the Chicago ballroom scene (aimed for POC queer individuals).
As a transgender woman, however, participating in postsecondary education and careers was difficult, so Miss Major turned to sex work (as many transwomen did
in the same position). So, Miss Major moved to New York to join the drag scene and perform as a showgirl, which then led to her participation in the Stonewall
Riots. After the events of Stonewall, she was placed in a psychiatric ward in Bellvue Hospital that isolated LGBTQ+ inmates to prevent the "corruption" of others.
After being released from the hospital, Miss Major saw the prevalence of anti-transgender violence after the murder of her friend, but also how the authorities
didn't care in solving such cases. Most of her activism became centered around protecting transgender individuals from such violence. In 1970, Miss Major was arrested
for robbing one of her clients and was later sent to Dannemora prison for wearing makeup during parole. In prison, Miss Major was forced into isolation, had her hair and
eyebrows shaved, and forced to walk naked. However, her spirit didn't break and through meeting leaders like Frank "Big Black" Smith, she was able to learn more and
participate in prison reform activism. Throughout the rest of her life, her activism focused on transgender treatment in prisons and HIV prevention for unhoused people.
Lucy Hicks Anderson (1886-1954)
Lucy Hicks Anderson was a woman from Kentucky who owned a brothel in Oxnard, California. In 1944, Lucy married Reuben Anderson, and a disease outbreak in Oxnard led to
the examination of Lucy Hicks as assigned male at birth. As a result, Lucy's marriage was voided and she was arrested for perjury, despite her identity proving she was
a woman. After being placed in probation for ten years, Lucy and her partner was tried and convinced for fraud, banning her from Oxnard. She moved to and lived in LA for
the rest of her life.
